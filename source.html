A TripCoil CSV is not a table ‚Äî it is a graph. Each row describes either a thing (PAN, DEV, TERM) or a connection (WIR, NET).

Core Row Types (ROW_TYPE)

The power of TC-S lies in its five primary row discriminators:

  • PAN (Panel): Defines the physical enclosure (The Namespace).
  • DEV (Device): Defines the equipment/hardware (Relays, Blocks, Switches).
  • TERM (Terminal): Defines the electrical "personality" of a specific pin or point.
  • WIR (Wire): Defines the physical conductor and its point-to-point path.
  • NET (Network): Defines global signal attributes (Line weights, colors, logic).
Click to view full column details
  1. ROW_TYPE: Discriminator (PAN, DEV, TERM, WIR, NET).
  2. PANEL: The physical enclosure owning the object.
  3. DEVICE_TAG: The unique ID (AA, TB1) within the panel.
  4. TERMINAL: The physical pin/terminal ID (C01, 10).
  5. TERM_KIND: The electrical logic (COIL, CONTACT_NO, CT, VT).
  6. ELEM_ID: Grouping ID for schematic symbols (e.g., Pins 1 & 2 = `K1_COIL`).
  7. DEVICE_PART: The catalog part number (SEL-421, UK5N).
  8. TO_PANEL: Destination enclosure for wires.
  9. TO_DEVICE: Destination equipment tag.
  10. TO_TERMINAL: Destination pin/terminal ID.
  11. CABLE_ID: Physical jacket/bundle identifier for multi-conductors.
  12. STATUS: The countdown of loose ends (**C** for confirmed, **1?**‚Äì**5?** for pending).
  13. SIGNAL_ID: The logical net name (**TRIP 1**, **CLOSE**, **52A**).
  14. REMARKS: Metadata, pseudonyms (**SUB_411L**), and wire color/gauge notes.

üñ•Ô∏è Using TripCoil-Terminal

TripCoil-Terminal (`index.html`) is the interactive CLI application used to create and verify your data. It runs directly in the browser and mimics a retro terminal environment.

1. The CLI Interface

The interface uses a prompt `>_` structure. You can type commands and press ENTER.

  • History: Use the Up/Down Arrow Keys to cycle through previous commands.
  • Mobile: On touch devices, use the green ‚àß / ‚à® buttons on the bottom-right.

2. Main Menu Modules

When you start the application, you are presented with the Main Menu.

Command Module Description
1 (TRACE) Trace Tool: The primary verification mode. Enter loop-back and branch data to confirm wiring status.
2 (PLOT) Plotter: Generates a visual schematic graph of your currently loaded CSV data.
3 (DATA) Data Interface: A Notepad interface to Copy/Paste CSV text directly into the system.
4 (SET) Settings: (Experimental) Configuration for terminal colors and behaviors.

3. Global Commands

These commands work from anywhere in the application.

  • SAVE: Downloads the current state as TC-Export.csv.
  • LOAD: Opens a file picker to import a .csv file.
  • CLEAR (or CLS): Wipes the terminal screen.
  • HELP (or ?): Shows the list of available commands.
  • QUIT (or EXIT): Returns to the Main Menu.
üí° Pro Tip: Safe Saving TripCoil operates in memory. If you close the tab, your work is lost. Use the SAVE command frequently to backup your progress to a file.

üîå 1 ‚Äî DEVICE DEFINITIONS TERM

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) language, the TERM row is the Interface Definition.

If the DEV row says, "A SEL-421 exists," the TERM rows say, "The SEL-421 has these specific pins, and this is what they do electrically." You only need to define a TERM row when you want the TripCoil-Plotter to understand the logic of a specific pin or when you want TripCoil-Terminal to know the purpose of that point.

üìù Note Here is how I envision you using the TERM row for different types of equipment:

1. The "Logic" Terminals (Contacts & Coils)

For devices like Lockout Relays (86) or Interposing Relays (K1), the TERM row tells the plotter which pins belong together to form a symbol.

Scenario: An ABB-86 lockout relay. Pins 1 & 2 are the Trip Coil. Pins 3 & 4 are a Normally Open contact.

The Logic: By giving pins 3 and 4 the same ELEM_ID, you tell the plotter they are a single "unit."

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND,       ELEM,    PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,     REMARKS
  TERM, 1A,  86G, 1,    COIL,       86_COIL, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     TRIP_BUS, Coil Start
  TERM, 1A,  86G, 2,    COIL,       86_COIL, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     DC_NEG,   Coil End
  TERM, 1A,  86G, 3,    CONTACT_NO, 86_CON1, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     GEN_TRIP, Contact A
  TERM, 1A,  86G, 4,    CONTACT_NO, 86_CON1, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     BKR_TRIP, Contact B

2. The "Analog" Terminals (CTs & VTs)

These are high-risk terminals. Using TERM_KIND allows for safety audits. If a wire carrying 125VDC lands on a terminal marked CT, your system can throw a red flag.

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND, ELEM,    PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,   REMARKS
  TERM, 1A,  AA,  Z01,  CT,   PH_A_CT, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     IA_POL, Phase A Polarity
  TERM, 1A,  AA,  Z02,  CT,   PH_A_CT, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     IA_NON, Phase A Non-Pol

3. The "Passive" Terminals (Terminal Blocks)

For a standard terminal block (TB1), you often don't need a TERM row for every single pin unless you want to define specific behavior like a "Shorting Block" for CTs.

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND,       ELEM,    PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN, REMARKS
  TERM, 1A,  TB1, 01,   SHORT_LINK, CT_JMP1, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     CT Phase A Shorting
  TERM, 1A,  TB1, 02,   SHORT_LINK, CT_JMP1, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     CT Phase A Shorting

Why use TERM rows instead of just WIR rows?

  • Template Creation: You can create a "Gold Standard" list of TERM rows for a SEL-421 once.
  • Schematic Accuracy: The TERM row is what dictates the symbol. A WIR row just tells the plotter "draw a line from A to B." The TERM row tells it "at Point A, draw a Normally Closed contact."
  • Physical Awareness: For TripCoil-Terminal, the TERM row can hold metadata like "Screw Torque" or "Wire Lug Type" in the REMARKS column.
‚ú® Implementation Tip: The "Library" Strategy Since you might have 50 terminals on a relay, you don't want to type 50 TERM rows every time. You can keep a "Master Library" CSV where you define the TERM rows for a SEL-421 once. Your final project CSV would only need to contain the WIR rows, and your script can "import" the terminal definitions based on the DEVICE_PART found in the DEV row.

üîó 1 ‚Äî DEVICE DEFINITIONS WIR

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) language, the WIR row is the Physical Conductor.

If the DEV row defines the "boxes" and the TERM row defines the "logic of the pins," the WIR row is the actual copper wire that connects point A to point B. This is the row that drives your TripCoil-Terminal list for field technicians and allows TripCoil-Plotter to draw the lines on the page.

1. The Anatomy of a WIR Row

The WIR row is unique because it is the only row type that uses the Destination columns (8, 9, and 10). It creates a bridge between two DEVICE_TAG objects.

2. Situation Snippets for WIR

A. Internal Panel Wiring (Point-to-Point)

This is a standard "jumper" or connection within the same enclosure.

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM,   KIND, ELEM, PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,   REMARKS
  WIR,  1A,  AA,  OUT101, ,     ,     ,     1A,   TB1,  12,   W102, ,     TRIP_A, #14 AWG RED

B. The "Field" Cable (Multi-conductor)

This represents a cable leaving the panel and going to a primary device, like a Breaker.

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND, ELEM, PART, TO_P,      TO_D, TO_T, CABL,     STAT, SIGN,     REMARKS
  WIR,  1A,  TB1, 12,   ,     ,     ,     BKR_FIELD, BKR1, TC1,  CBL_BKR1, ,     TRIP_A,   Cond 1
  WIR,  1A,  TB1, 13,   ,     ,     ,     BKR_FIELD, BKR1, 52A,  CBL_BKR1, ,     BKR_STAT, Cond 2

C. The "Jumper" (Shorting internal to a device)

Sometimes you need to daisy-chain power between terminals on the same device.

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND, ELEM, PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,   REMARKS
  WIR,  1A,  AA,  A02,  ,     ,     ,     1A,   AA,   A04,  JMP1, ,     DC_POS, Internal Relay Jumper

3. Why WIR is the "Source of Truth" for your Tools

For TripCoil-Terminal:

Your terminal app can "pivot" this data. If a technician is looking at Terminal Block TB1-12, the app searches for TB1 and 12 in any of the From/To columns.

For TripCoil-Plotter:

The plotter looks at the SIGNAL_ID (e.g., TRIP_A). It finds every WIR row with that ID and draws a continuous logical line. Even if there are 5 physical `WIR` segments, the Plotter knows they are all the same Logic Net.

üí° Golden Rule of TripCoil Every WIR row must:
  1. Reference a real source terminal (PANEL + DEVICE + TERMINAL)
  2. Reference a real destination terminal (TO_PANEL + TO_DEVICE + TO_TERMINAL)
‚ö†Ô∏è Rule of One Direction To keep your CSV clean, it is best practice to only list a wire once.
  • Bad: Having one row for `AA -> TB1` and another row for `TB1 -> AA`.
  • Good: Always list wires from the "Source" (Relay/Logic) to the "Destination" (Terminal Block/Field) or follow a consistent hierarchy (Panel -> Device -> Field).

üì° 1 ‚Äî DEVICE DEFINITIONS NET

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) ecosystem, the NET row is the Logical Configuration.

If WIR defines the physical copper, the NET row defines the "rules" for the signal traveling through that copper. Think of it as the Style Sheet for your electrical signals.

Without NET, TripCoil is a wiring tracker. With NET, TripCoil becomes a digital twin of the protection system.

1. The Anatomy of a NET Row

The NET row doesn't care about specific terminals. It only cares about the SIGNAL_ID.

  • ROW_TYPE: `NET`
  • SIGNAL_ID: The unique name used in your `WIR` and `TERM` rows (e.g., `TRIP_A`).
  • TERM_KIND: General classification for the signal (e.g., `DC_POWER`, `AC_VOLTAGE`, `COMMS`).
  • REMARKS: The "Settings" or "Attributes" (e.g., `COLOR=RED, GAUGE=12, LAYER=TRIP_LOGIC`).

2. Why do you need NET?

NET turns a wiring CSV into a renderable schematic.

Example A: Power Distribution

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND,     ELEM, PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,   REMARKS
  NET,  ,    ,    ,     DC_POWER, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     DC_POS, COLOR=RED; GAUGE=10; DASHED=FALSE

Example B: CT Circuits

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND,      ELEM, PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,   REMARKS
  NET,  ,    ,    ,     CT_SIGNAL, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     IA_POL, COLOR=ORANGE; WEIGHT=2.0; LAYER=ANALOG

Example C: Internal Logic

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND,  ELEM, PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN,       REMARKS
  NET,  ,    ,    ,     LOGIC, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     67P_PICKUP, COLOR=BLUE; GAUGE=22; LAYER=PROTECTION

3. How the TripCoil Tools use NET

For TripCoil-Plotter: When the plotter prepares to draw a line for SIGNAL_ID: TRIP_A, it finds COLOR=RED and sets the digital pen to Red.

For TripCoil-Terminal: Your terminal app can say: "This is Signal TRIP_A. According to the NET definition, this should be a RED 14AWG wire." This acts as a built-in error check.

üóÑÔ∏è 2 ‚Äî PANEL DEFINITIONS

The Panel (PAN) row acts as the highest-level container. It is the physical "envelope" for everything else. While you can define devices without a specific panel row, having one allows you to define the physical attributes of the enclosure itself.

1. The Role of the PAN Row

The PAN row defines the environment. It is the "Root" of your hierarchy.

2. The Namespace Concept (Critical)

The Panel name is what prevents data collisions. In a substation, you might have ten devices labeled "AA" or "TB1."

  • 1A:AA and 2B:AA are treated as completely different physical objects.
  • Whenever TripCoil-Terminal generates a wiring list, it uses the PANEL column to filter down to exactly what the technician sees when they open a single cabinet door.

3. Example Panel Definition Snippet

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND, ELEM, PART,    TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN, REMARKS
  PAN,  1A,  ,    ,     ,     ,     ,        ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     Primary Relay Panel
  PAN,  1B,  ,    ,     ,     ,     ,        ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     Auxiliary Logic Panel
  DEV,  1A,  AA,  ,     ,     ,     SEL-421, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     Main Relay (Located in 1A)
  DEV,  1B,  AA,  ,     ,     ,     SEL-421, ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     Back-up Relay (Located in 1B)

5. Managing Large Panels

When you have a panel with 500+ wires, the PAN row becomes your best friend for Database Integrity. You can run a script to "Sum the Panel" to find total devices, jumpers, and cables.

üè∑Ô∏è 3 ‚Äî DEVICE TAG DEFINITION

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) schema, the Device Tag (Index 3) is the unique "address" of a component within its panel.

1. The "Namespace" Rule

The Device Tag is not globally unique; it is unique within its Panel. Panel 1A + Tag AA = Relay 1. Panel 2B + Tag AA = Relay 2.

2. Standardized Tagging Strategy

  • AA - ZZ: Protection Relays
  • TB: Terminal Blocks
  • K: Interposing Relays
  • SW / FT: Switches / Test Switches
  • CB / F: Breakers / Fuses
  • RTAC / COMM: Communications

4. Handling "Pseudonyms" vs. "Tags"

In TripCoil-Source, the DEVICE_TAG (AA) is the Physical Label on the equipment, while the pseudonym belongs in the REMARKS column. 

# TYPE, PAN, TAG, TERM, KIND, ELEM, PART, TO_P, TO_D, TO_T, CABL, STAT, SIGN, REMARKS
  DEV,  1A,  AA,  ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     SUB_411L (Functional Pseudonym)
  WIR,  1A,  AA,  ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     ,     Wire landed on physical tag AA
üõ°Ô∏è Validation Check The most important rule for Device Tags in your CSV system is: Every DEVICE_TAG used in a WIR or TERM row must have a corresponding DEV row defining what it is.

üìç 4 ‚Äî TERMINAL DEFINITION

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) schema, the Terminal (Index 4) is the specific "Point of Contact" on a device. It is the most granular level of the physical world.

1. The Anatomy of a Terminal String

  • Alpha-Numeric (Relays): `A01`, `C24`, `Z05`
  • Hardware-Point (RTUs/IO): `X1-1`, `TB2-10`
  • Polarity-Based (CTs/VTs): `X1`, `X2`, `H1`, `H2`
  • Functional (Switches): `1`, `2`, `3`, `4`

2. Terminal Logic in Different Row Types

ROW_TYPE How TERMINAL is used
DEV Ignored. The device row defines the whole "box," not individual pins.
TERM Defined. This is where you declare that "Terminal A01" on "Device AA" is a `CONTACT_NO`.
WIR Connected. This is the physical landing point for the conductor.

3. Handling the "Terminal Block" (TB) Exception

I recommend a Prefix-Number format (e.g., `TB1-01`). This makes the CSV much easier to search. If you search for "1", you get every device. If you search for "TB1-01", you get exactly that point.

üß¨ 5 ‚Äî TERMINAL KIND

The "Electrical Intelligence" of the system. While the Terminal ID is just a label, the TERM_KIND tells TripCoil-Plotter which symbol to draw and TripCoil-Terminal what safety rules to apply.

1. The Standardized Vocabulary

  • COIL: Relay or Contactor coil.
  • CONTACT_NO: Normally Open Contact.
  • CONTACT_NC: Normally Closed Contact.
  • CONTACT_CO: Change-over (Form C).
  • DI: Digital Input (Optoisolated).
  • DO: Digital Output (Transistor).
  • CT: Current Transformer Input.
  • VT: Voltage Transformer Input.
  • PWR_POS / PWR_NEG: DC/AC Power Supply.
  • GND: Chassis or Earth Ground.
  • FT_SW: Test Switch Blade.

üîó 6 ‚Äî ELEMENT ID

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) schema, ELEM_ID (Index 6) is the "Associative Logic." It is what groups individual terminals into a single, recognizable component on a schematic.

1. The "Grouping" Concept

A single physical device (like a relay or a lockout) often contains multiple functional parts. By giving multiple pins the same ELEM_ID, you tell the plotter they are a single "unit."

3. Naming Conventions for ELEM_ID

  • COIL_1, COIL_2: Physical coils.
  • CON_1, CON_2...: Generic contacts.
  • TRIP_OUT, CLOSE_OUT: High-speed outputs.
  • IA_PHASE, IB_PHASE: CT inputs.
  • FT_1, FT_2: Test switches.

üìë 7 ‚Äî DEVICE PART

The "Catalog Link." It bridges the gap between a generic tag (like `AA`) and the physical reality of the equipment (like a `SEL-421-0-1-2-3`).

1. The Role of the DEV Row

The DEVICE_PART column is primarily used in ROW_TYPE: DEV. It acts as a pointer to a library or a manual.

2. Standardizing Part Numbers

Include the "Base Model" followed by the specific "Order Code" if it affects the terminal layout (e.g., `SEL-421-4`, `ELECTRO-SERIES-24`, `PHOENIX-UK5N`).

3. The "Library Lookup" Logic

The power of `DEVICE_PART` comes from Inheritance. You define the `TERM` rows for a `SEL-421` once in a library, and any device using that part number inherits those definitions.

üö™ 8 ‚Äî TO PANEL

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) schema, TO_PANEL (Index 8) represents the Physical Boundary. It is the first coordinate of the destination address for a wire.

1. Defining the Scope of the "Jump"

  • Internal Wire: If `PANEL` is `1A` and `TO_PANEL` is `1A`, the wire never leaves the cabinet.
  • External Wire: If `PANEL` is `1A` and `TO_PANEL` is `BKR_502`, the wire leaves the panel through a gland or conduit.
üö´ Data Integrity: The "Return Path" A core rule of the TC-S language is that the TO_PANEL must be a valid panel name that exists either in your current CSV or in a "Master Site Map."

üéØ 9 ‚Äî TO DEVICE

The specific destination "Object" at the other end of the wire. While `TO_PANEL` gets you to the right room or cabinet, `TO_DEVICE` tells you exactly which piece of equipment you are walking toward.

5. Validation: The "Ghost Device" Check

One of the most powerful features of your TC-S language is the ability to run a Ghost Check. Every tag listed in `TO_DEVICE` must exist as a `DEVICE_TAG` in a `DEV` row somewhere in your project. This prevents "orphaned" wires that lead to nowhere.

üìå 10 ‚Äî TO TERMINAL

The "Landing Point." It is the final coordinate in the physical address string. While `TO_DEVICE` tells you which box to look at, `TO_TERMINAL` tells you exactly which screw to turn.

2. Why this is the "Commissioning Key"

For your TripCoil-Terminal application, this column is the most important piece of data for a field technician. It enables Point-to-Point Verification and Continuity Testing.

5. Validation: The "Physicality" Check

A validation script can perform a Terminal Existence Check. It looks up the `DEVICE_PART` for that device and checks a master library to see if `TO_TERMINAL` actually exists on that model.

üßµ 11 ‚Äî CABLE ID

The Physical Bundle Identifier. While the `WIR` row describes a single conductor, the `CABLE_ID` tells your system which conductors are physically bound together inside the same jacket.

2. Why this is Critical for TripCoil-Terminal

When a technician is working in the field, they aren't looking for a "Trip Wire"‚Äîthey are looking for "Cable CBL_BKR1." This allows grouping wires by cable pull, managing spares, and printing cable labels.

5. Naming Conventions for Cable IDs

  • W01, W02...: Internal panel wire bundles.
  • C01, C02...: Control cables (Multi-conductor).
  • P01, P02...: Power cables (High current).
  • FO01, FO02...: Fiber Optic cables.

üö¶ 12 ‚Äî STATUS

The Status (Index 12) handles up to 5 pending wires. In substation panels, you can easily have multiple conductors stacked on a single terminal point. By using the NUM? status as a countdown, you create a high-resolution "Punch List."

1. The 5-Wire Status Hierarchy

  • C (Confirmed): 0 wires pending. The terminal is fully landed and verified.
  • 1? to 5?: The number of conductors still unverified or loose at this point.

3. Workflow Example: The "Countdown"

  1. Morning: The CSV shows `5?`. The technician sees a bundle of 5 wires.
  2. Mid-Day: They land 3 wires. They update the CSV to `2?`.
  3. End of Day: The last two are landed and verified. The status updates to `C`.

üì∂ 13 ‚Äî SIGNAL ID

This is where the "Logic" (Schematic) meets the "Copper" (Wiring). Common substation signals like TRIP 1, TRIP 2, CLOSE, and 52A behave as logical nets.

3. How the Signals behave in your Tools

For TripCoil-Plotter: The Plotter sees `SIGNAL_ID: 52A` and `TERM_KIND: DI`, draws a Digital Input symbol, and connects it to the breaker status contact across the page.

For TripCoil-Terminal: When a technician is working on `TB1-10`, the app shows "Signal: TRIP 1". If they see a wire labeled "CLOSE", they know they have a physical error.

üìù 14 ‚Äî REMARKS

In the TripCoil-Source (TC-S) schema, REMARKS (Index 14) is the "Elastic Layer." It is the catch-all column for metadata that doesn't fit into a rigid logical or physical category.

1. The Role of REMARKS across Row Types

  • PAN: Physical location or GPS notes.
  • DEV: Pseudonyms / Functional names.
  • TERM: Pin-specific notes (e.g., "Polarity end").
  • WIR: Wire color / Gauge / Type.
  • NET: Global signal notes.
Why this is powerful: Your TripCoil-Plotter can be set to "Alias Mode." It looks at the `WIR` row for `AA`, jumps to the `DEV` row for `AA`, pulls `SUB_411L` from the Remarks, and uses that name on the schematic instead of the generic "AA."
Special Indicator: ! If you see a ! (Exclamation Point), it means the wire has been confirmed on one terminal but not the other end (Single-Ended Trace).
